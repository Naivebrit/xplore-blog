    Working on Track Layout

    Nick, Dan, Adam, and Celeste reviewed the coaster track layouts and ironed out some of the details.   • Post 9 - 10/15/17

    Preparing for the Webinar

    Today our group came together with one goal in mind, to create the frame. We acquired 2x4 dimensional wood from our local Menards along with some screws and got to work. After updating our design several times we were able to come up with one the we thought was feasible for the Zoom! Coaster.   • Post 10 - 10/22/17

    Frame Design

    After discussing with our advisors we came up with a better alternative to the frame. We decided to make it out of 80-20 metal stock for extra support and make it look more appealing. This will be a decision in the long run as it will make our lives a lot easier when storing, presenting, and shipping our final project. The design of the frame will still look very similar to the wooden one we initially made.


  • Post 11 - 11/7/17

    Frame Complete

    We created the entire frame using the cut pieces of 80-20 we acquired to create a frame that suits the needs of our project. It dimensions fit the requirements for shipping and can still fit the rollercoaster track and PLC we need it to. We also used sheet aluminum for the base. The middle piece of sheet aluminum was cut with a sheet metal shear to make it the exact dimensions we need it to be.


  • Post 12 - 11/9/17

  • Post 13 - 11/16/17

    Building the track pt 2

    The first hills of the track were created today and work really well with the roller coaster car we have. We have attached aluminum stock supports in order to keep the track in place. This helps the track stay put postionionally and keep the correct orientation.


  • Post 14 - 11/20/17

    Machining the cart

    One of the ideas we had for our cart was to have it be made of aluminum so the extra weight would have it go down the track even faster. This would minimize the loss of energy as the cart would go down the track. However, we discovered that it is extremely difficult to properly machine the cart if it is made of metal. So we stuck with 3d printing PLA for our cart.


  • Post 15 - 11/27/17

    Building the track pt 3

    More and more of the track was built including a loop. We made the loop the biggest dimension it could be while the cart could go around it. After bending the acrylic into the desired shape the loop functioned very well. It retained enough energy for the cart to continue around the track.


  • Post 16 - 11/29/17

    Troubleshooting the cart

    Another issue with the cart was discovered. It tended to get stuck on parts of the track and could not go around a corkscrew piece almost at all. We quickly found a way to remedy the problem by making the cart into two smaller pieces connected by a wire. We later tested a ball & socket mechanism only to find it did not work well either. The solution that we came up with that works the best was connecting the pieces with pneumatic tubing. This made the cart go around not only the loop super well but the corkscrew piece as well.
